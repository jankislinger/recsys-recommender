Key Objectives:

    1. Categorization Accuracy: Ensure each article is placed in the most appropriate categories based on its content, themes, and relevance.
    2. Category Utility: Each category should contain a sufficient number of articles to be useful for navigation. Avoid creating categories with too few articles.
    3. User-Centric Design: Prioritize categories that align with user interests and search behaviors, making the interface intuitive and efficient.
    4. Consistency: Maintain consistent categorization logic across all articles to avoid confusion and ensure a cohesive user experience.

Guidelines:

    - Multiple Categories: Assign each article to at least 8-12 categories to enhance discoverability.
    - Balanced Distribution: Aim for a balanced distribution of articles across categories to prevent overcrowding in some and underrepresentation in others.
    - Dynamic Updates: Be prepared to adjust categories as new articles are added or user needs evolve.
    - Clear Labels: Use clear, descriptive, and user-friendly category names that accurately reflect the content within.

Example Workflow:

    - Analyze the article's title and abstract to identify its core themes.
    - Map the article to existing categories or propose new ones if necessary.
    - Ensure the article is placed in categories that are both specific and broad enough to be useful.
    - Review the categorization to confirm it aligns with user needs and interface goals.

Examples of Categories:

    - Family of algorithm (e.g., Matrix Factorization, Encoder, Multi-Armed Bandits, etc.).
    - Domain of the problem (e.g., Books, Movies, Healthcare, Education, etc.).
    - Something specific in the recommendation space (e.g. Cold Start)
    - Aspects of evaluation (e.g. Diversity of Recommendations, Beyond Accuracy)
    - Whether there are any real word applications (e.g. A/B Test, User Survey, etc.)
    - Any other type of categorization that seems relevant to the article.

Output:

    - Return just the list of categories.
    - The format should be a list of keywords separated by comma, e.g. "Scalability, Web Systems, Implicit Feedback"


Title: Is ChatGPT Fair for Recommendation? Evaluating Fairness in Large Language Model Recommendation
Abstract:
The resounding triumph of the Large Language Models (LLMs) has ushered in a novel LLM for recommendation (LLM4rec) paradigm. Notwithstanding, the capacity of LLM4rec to provide equitable recommendations remains uncharted due to the potential presence of societal prejudices in LLMs. In order to avert the plausible hazard of employing LLM4rec, we scrutinize the fairness of LLM4rec with respect to the usersâ€™ sensitive attributes. Owing to the disparity between LLM4rec and the conventional recommendation paradigm, there are challenges in utilizing the conventional recommendation fairness benchmark directly. To explore the fairness of recommendations under the LLM4rec, we propose a new benchmark Fairness in Large language models for Recommendation (FairLR), which consists of carefully designed metrics and a dataset that considers eight sensitive attributes in two recommendation scenarios: music and movie. We utilize our FairLR benchmark to examine ChatGPT and expose that it still demonstrates bias towards certain sensitive attributes while making recommendations. Our code and dataset can be found at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/FairLR-751D/.
